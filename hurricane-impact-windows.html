Florida guide

Hurricane impact windows & doors

What Florida homeowners need to know before they buy.

Why impact-rated products matter

When Hurricane Andrew devastated South Florida in 1992, conventional windows failed catastrophically — allowing wind pressure into homes and causing roofs to lift off. Florida responded with some of the toughest building codes in the world. Today, windows and doors in coastal and HVHZ areas must withstand wind-borne debris impact or be protected by approved shutters.

Impact-rated products use laminated glass: two panes bonded with a tough interlayer that holds together even when the outer pane cracks. That keeps wind and rain out and maintains the building envelope during a storm.

The High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ)

Miami-Dade and Broward counties form the HVHZ — the strictest code zone in Florida. Products installed here must pass large-missile impact tests and receive a Notice of Acceptance (NOA) from Miami-Dade County. Not every window sold in Florida is HVHZ-rated; specifying the wrong product can fail inspection.

AOA partners with manufacturers whose product lines carry HVHZ approvals, including PGT WinGuard, ES Windows, CWS, and WinDoor.

Impact vs. non-impact: which do you need?

If you're in the HVHZ or a coastal wind-borne debris region, impact-rated products are typically required for new construction and often for replacements. Inland counties may allow non-impact windows with shutter systems — but many homeowners choose impact anyway for convenience (no shutter deployment) and insurance benefits.
